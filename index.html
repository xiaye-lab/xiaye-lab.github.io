I am a PhD student at the National University of Singapore (NUS) in the College of Design and Engineering, working within the City Syntax Lab. My research focuses on urban sustainability, building decarbonization, and spatial analytics. More details here »

With a focus on data-driven methodologies, my work aims to unlock the potential of urban data to accelerate the energy transition in high-density cities. I leverage machine learning (specifically Graph Neural Networks), large-scale geospatial analysis, and agent-based modeling to address scientific and practical challenges in creating zero-carbon, equitable urban environments.

Research

My research sits at the intersection of the built environment and computational technologies. I am currently developing Graph Neural Network (UrbanGNN) models to analyze spatial spillovers and the trade-offs between Environmental and Societal Performance Indices in Singapore. Additionally, I am designing multi-agent systems using the OpenClaw framework to simulate and manage energy consumption and thermal comfort across the NUS campus. View my code repositories »

Publications

  • Xia, Y., et al. (2025). The optimal buffer for urban features to impact outdoor thermal comfort. Sustainable Cities and Society.
    This paper aims to answer one question: Within which buffer, and in what ways, do urban features impact outdoor thermal comfort (OTC) across diverse urban settings most effectively? To address this question, the research adopts a buffer-dependent workflow consisting of five steps: (1) Cooler Area Searching; (2) OTC Simulation; (3) Cross-scale Validation; (4) Cooler Area Identification; and (5) Space Design Analysis. At the methodology's core is a series of machine learning tools (e.g., Bayesian-optimized CatBoost models and Shapley Additive exPlanations) used to identify the optimal buffer and interpret the urban features that matter. The results showed that an optimal buffer range of 30 to 50 m accurately captures the thermal impacts of urban features across eight typical local climate zones (LCZs). Notably, urban features’ critical impact on OTC was highlighted, including percentage of view (PV), orientation of view (OV), average height index (AHI), and vegetation coverage (VE). For the first time, this research articulates the optimal buffer for urban features to maximize OTC in different LCZs. The findings provide buffer-sensitive microclimates interventions and content-specific strategies for urban designers and policymakers to address the rampant threat of heat waves and foster sustainable development.
  • Xia, Y., et al. (2025). Understanding the impacts of space design on local outdoor thermal comfort: An approach combining DepthmapX and XGBoost. Energy and Buildings.
    Researchers and designers alike are keen to explore proper space designs to alleviate the heat waves. A niche research area of this type focuses on the smaller scale, local communities to achieve the so-called ‘cool spots’ for residents. However, despite the momentum gained so far, it remains unclear what the key design elements are (e.g., space orientation, aspect ratio, or building density) and how they interact with each other in impacting outdoor thermal comfort (OTC), particularly in some complex, vertical ‘spots’. This research aims to provide an improved understanding of cool spot reasoning by proposing a new paradigm to engage DepthmapX (an analytic tool for urban spatial configuration), XGBoost (a machine learning tool), and on-site verification. By implementing the paradigm on a university campus in Hong Kong for the case study, it was discovered that Percentage of View (PV), Average Height Index (AHI), and Connectivity (CON) are the three most influential factors leading to the formation of a ‘cool spot’. DepthmapX can not only help quantify space designs but also help translate the indexes back to real-life space design options. The XGBoost can help better interpret the pathway from different space design indexes to different OTC but more explainable causal relationships are desired. This research advanced our understanding of the impacts of different space designs on OTC and provided references to designers in achieving OTC in smaller--scale, local communities. It also opens a new avenue to understand the causal relationships in a more detailed and explainable fashion.
